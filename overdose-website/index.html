Cocktails That Kill:

Lethal Drug Combinations in Allegheny County

One of the most common misconceptions about the overdose epidemic is the assumption that people are overdosing by taking just one drug. In reality, just 89 of Allegheny County's 738 lethal overdoses - 12 percent - were attributed to a single substance. Many of these deaths could have been prevented with harm reduction education or regulations designed to eliminate undisclosed cutting agents.

This project explores five of the most deadly drugs in the county - alcohol, cocaine, alprazolam (xanax), heroin, and fentanyl - to see which other drugs to those deaths. We've created a series of visualizations to examine three major questions: How many drugs were combined? Which drugs were combined? When were they combined?

Artist Statements

Angelica Walker

As a group, we quickly agreed that we wanted to choose a topic centered around Pittsburgh. I suggested a dataset from the Western Pennsylvania Regional Data Center website that lists out all the fatal drug overdoses in Allegheny County. This dataset contained data for all 424 closed cases of 2016 drug overdoses. Each case included the date, time, drugs used, age of the user, sex of the user, and zip code of the user as well as zip code of the death.
Because there was so much data, we spent a long time deciding exactly what we wanted to visualize. While we were trying to figure out what to do, Cindy asked why there were multiple drugs listed next to the same person. The rest of the group members admitted they were also confused. I explained to them that most people overdose by taking a combination they don’t usually take, or taking drugs secretly tainted with other drugs. This confusion was what led me to focus in on combinations.
The first thing I did for the project was edit my Python script from Synthesis 2 to calculate things for this project. I created two different programs: od_counter.py to count the number of drugs combined for overdoses in each of the five major categories (alcohol, cocaine, xanax, heroin, and fentanyl) and combo_counter.py to count the number of times any given drug was used in an overdose.
The next thing I did was design the website. We worked as a group to write up some introduction text, draw up a mockup of what we wanted the website to look like, choose colors for the graphs and the website, and decide where we wanted each graph to go. We decided that creating buttons for each of the five major drugs would be a good source of interactivity.
Again, I leveraged resources from my old projects as a backbone for the new website. I also used a button rollover effect that I found at the beginning of the semester on Github, but never had the opportunity to use until now. Lastly, after trying out all the normal websafe fonts and not getting the result I wanted, I looked into other options and found the Google Fonts API. I found a cool font called Stardos Stencil and put it in the CSS for body, so it would automatically appear in every section of the website.
The hardest part of this project for me was creating the actual pie chart. I originally wanted to somehow showcase the drugs that went into each individual case, but couldn’t think of any chart that would do that. I began trying to make a simple pie chart in Matplotlib, but couldn’t figure out how to upload CSV data files to the Python code. Since all of my other datasets were so small, I didn’t know how to interact with such a large dataset.
I asked my group members for help, and they suggested trying Plot.ly. This proved to work much better for our purposes, and I was able to quickly create each of the graphs and paste the code into our website.
I was really happy with how we communicated and worked together as a team. Group projects are usually something I fear, but everything about this project went smoothly. I feel like each of us contributed to the best of our abilities and brought something unique to the table, and this led to a final project that each of us can be proud of.

Katie Osborne

For this assignment, we decided we wanted to focus on something local and important, so we took a look at some of the public datasets available for Allegheny County to see what piqued our interest. After some discussion, we decided to analyze overdose data from 2016 to understand a bit more about drug overdoses that affect our area. What we found most interesting was the fact that the majority of overdose deaths were due to a combination of drugs as opposed to just one drug, which is something that I hadn’t really thought about at all before this project.
The majority of overdose deaths were due to alcohol, cocaine, Xanax, heroin, and fentanyl in combination with one or more other drugs taken at the same time. Because there are so many drugs used in combination with these main five, we decided to make a few different charts to visualize drug usage among these overdose deaths.
As someone who hasn’t really had any interaction with drug use or overdoses before, I think being able to look at the different drugs used and understand the different cocktails that led to so many deaths is really important. With so much content to analyze, we really had to think about the best way to visualize the data. We ended up decided to make a pie chart showing the drugs used in combination with each the main five drugs so that it was easy to see which combinations were most common for each category. To further illustrate the use of multiple drugs among overdose victims, we also made bar charts showing how many drugs were used per death. We wanted to make sure that what we created would be easy to understand for someone who doesn’t know much about drug overdoses, and I think we achieved this goal.
Beyond just looking at the combination of drugs though, I also wanted to look at when the deaths were occurring. Our dataset included the date of death so I took the data and split it up by drug to create graphs over time showing overdoses on that drug (whether in combination or on its own). The data that we had for 2016 didn’t include many closed cases for December so the charts I made show the deaths for each of the five main drugs per month from January through November. To make my charts, I used Plotly, which I’ve never actually used before but that proved to be a pretty cool program that helped us create charts that are pretty and understandable. At first, I was using matplotlib and Python script to create the graphs, but my group members suggested I try the Plotly application and that made it much easier to create our visualizations.
What I found when I put the data into a graph was that there were a lot more overdoses during the warmer months, particularly in August, and fewer overdoses in the cold, winter months. I was at first surprised by this outcome because I expected deaths to be more frequent during the winter as people tend to be more depressed and unhappy during those months and may therefore be more likely to “dope up”. However, it actually makes a lot of sense that warmer months would have more overdoses because when it’s warm outside, people are more likely to get out of the house and go to parties and hang out with friends which is often the site of these tragic, accidental deaths. As a team, we were each able to take an aspect of this dataset and create visualizations that, when put together, illustrate the realities of drug abuse more clearly and highlight how important a topic it is to be aware of.
