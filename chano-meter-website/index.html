The Evolution of Chance:

An analysis of key themes in 10 Day, Acid Rap, and Coloring Book

10 Day

Released April 3, 2012

10 Day was Chance's first full-length mixtape. The songs were written during Chance's final days high school, while he was serving on a 10-day suspension for possessing marijuana on campus. After writing the songs of 10 Day, Chance dropped out of school to begin working on the album full-time. One line explains, "Fun fact: I'm never going back to school. Been there, done that."

In 10 Day, Chance raps about his transition from everyday student to local celebrity. In Long Time Part II, he sings, "Things ain’t been the same since Ms. Patterson called me famous." In Prom Night, he rapped about missing events like prom and graduation, and listening to teachers sneer at his musical dreams: "You made a mixtape? Good job, I hope you get a good job." At this point, Chance had a lot to prove to his teachers, family, peers.

This album detailed Chance's life on the South Side of Chicago, with references to money, cops, and guns. However, it didn't portray the gangster lifestyle in a very positive light; in Missing You, Chance raps, "Brown boys are dying and none of 'em were for business, and, all of 'em love they mamas and all of they mamas miss 'em." This was the beginning of Chance's long fight against violence in the streets of Chicago. More than anything, this was an album full of hope, for Chance's future as well as his city's.
